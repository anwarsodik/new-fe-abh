# Analyses

This section compares the GDPR and Indonesia’s Personal Data Protection Law (UUPDP) across the five legal dimensions set out above: scope, lawful bases for processing, data subject rights, exceptions and restrictions, and institutional design and enforcement. The aim is to show where the two regimes align, where they diverge, and what those differences imply for the protection of individuals and for the balance of power between data subjects, controllers, and the state.

---

## 1. Scope: Material and Territorial Reach

**GDPR.** The Regulation defines “personal data” as any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person; identifiability can rest on identifiers such as name, identification number, location data, or factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural, or social identity of that person. The definition is deliberately broad and technology-neutral. “Processing” covers any operation or set of operations performed on personal data, whether automated or not, including collection, recording, organisation, structuring, storage, adaptation, retrieval, consultation, use, disclosure, dissemination, alignment, combination, restriction, erasure, or destruction. The material scope of the GDPR is wide: it applies to processing carried out by controllers or processors established in the Union, regardless of whether the processing takes place in the EU. It also applies to controllers or processors outside the EU when their activities relate to the offering of goods or services to data subjects in the Union or to the monitoring of those data subjects’ behaviour within the Union. Certain activities are excluded or limited, notably processing for national security, law enforcement (covered by a separate directive), and purely personal or household activity.

**UUPDP.** Indonesia’s law adopts a similarly broad notion of personal data: data relating to an identified or identifiable natural person, whether directly or indirectly. The law also singles out “specific” or “sensitive” data (e.g. health, biometric, political views, religious beliefs, criminal record) for stricter treatment. Processing is defined in a way that largely mirrors the GDPR—encompassing collection, use, disclosure, storage, and destruction, among other operations. The territorial and personal scope of the UUPDP is tied to controllers and processors that are established in Indonesia or that process personal data of Indonesian residents in a manner that has legal effect or impacts them in Indonesia. The law thus extends to some processing abroad when it affects individuals in Indonesia, though the exact boundaries are less developed in the text and in practice than under the GDPR’s “targeting” and “monitoring” criteria.

**Comparison.** Both instruments treat “personal data” and “processing” in expansive terms, so that new technologies and uses are captured without needing constant legislative amendment. The main differences lie in territorial scope and in the treatment of sensitive data. The GDPR’s extraterritorial arm is well known and has been tested in enforcement; the UUPDP’s equivalent is newer and will depend on how the supervisory authority and courts interpret “legal effect” and “impact.” The GDPR does not use a separate category of “sensitive data” in the definition of personal data but instead lists “special categories” that are subject to additional conditions for lawful processing; the UUPDP’s explicit category of “specific” personal data serves a similar function. Overall, the legal issue of *what* is protected is addressed in a convergent way; the issue of *who* is bound, and in what situations, shows more divergence and will require further interpretation in Indonesia.

## 2. Lawful Bases for Processing

**GDPR.** Under Article 6(1), processing is lawful only if and to the extent that at least one of the following applies: the data subject has given consent; processing is necessary for the performance of a contract or for pre-contractual steps; processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation; processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject or another person; processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority; or processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the interests or fundamental rights of the data subject. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous; it must be demonstrated by a clear affirmative action. Where there is a clear imbalance of power—notably between employer and employee—consent may not be a valid basis. For “special categories” of data (e.g. health, biometric, political opinions), Article 9 provides a closed list of additional grounds, with consent and a number of necessity-based exceptions.

**UUPDP.** The Indonesian law also requires a lawful basis for processing. It recognises consent as one basis, along with other grounds that correspond broadly to contract performance, legal obligation, vital interests, public interest or official authority, and legitimate interests. The exact wording and the conditions attached to each basis are not identical to the GDPR; in particular, the way “legitimate interest” is framed and the extent to which it can be invoked by the state or by powerful private actors may differ. Consent under the UUPDP must be given voluntarily, specifically, and with adequate information; the law acknowledges that consent may not be valid in situations of imbalance. For “specific” (sensitive) personal data, the UUPDP imposes additional requirements and limits the circumstances in which such data may be processed.

**Comparison.** Structurally, the two regimes are aligned: both reject the idea that personal data can be processed without a recognised legal basis, and both list consent alongside several non-consent grounds. The devil is in the detail. The GDPR’s extensive body of guidance and case law on “legitimate interest,” “necessity,” and the invalidity of consent in unequal relationships has no direct parallel in Indonesia yet. How the UUPDP’s supervisory authority and courts will interpret “voluntary” consent when the controller is the state—for example in the context of digital identity or public services—will be decisive. If consent is treated as valid whenever a form is signed or a box ticked, the formal convergence with the GDPR will mask a substantive gap. The same applies to “public interest” and “official authority”: the broader and more loosely defined these are in practice, the more the UUPDP will diverge from the GDPR’s emphasis on necessity and proportionality.

## 3. Data Subject Rights

**GDPR.** The Regulation grants data subjects a set of rights that controllers must facilitate: the right of access (to confirmation of processing and to a copy of the data); the right to rectification of inaccurate data; the right to erasure (“right to be forgotten”) in specified circumstances; the right to restriction of processing; the right to data portability (where processing is based on consent or contract and is carried out by automated means); the right to object to processing based on legitimate interest or on direct marketing; and rights in relation to automated decision-making and profiling, including the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing that significantly affects the data subject, unless exceptions apply. Controllers must respond within one month, extendable in complex cases; they must inform the data subject of the possibility of lodging a complaint with a supervisory authority and of seeking a judicial remedy. Restrictions on these rights are permitted only when provided for by law and when they respect the essence of the right and are proportionate.

**UUPDP.** The Indonesian law grants a similar catalogue of rights: access and correction (rectification); erasure and restriction; portability (in certain conditions); objection; and rights related to automated decision-making. The procedures and time limits for responding may differ from the GDPR; the law also specifies the obligations of controllers to provide information and to facilitate the exercise of rights. As under the GDPR, the exercise of rights can be subject to restrictions when laid down by law, including for reasons of national security, public order, or other overriding interests.

**Comparison.** On paper, the UUPDP brings Indonesian law close to the GDPR in terms of the *types* of rights recognised. The comparative question is whether the rights are equally *effective*. Effectiveness depends on several factors: the clarity of the procedures (how to submit a request, to whom, and within what time); the capacity and willingness of the supervisory authority to support data subjects and to sanction non-compliance; the availability of affordable and accessible judicial redress; and the extent of statutory restrictions. In the EU, the one-month response deadline and the possibility of complaint to an independent authority have created a baseline that many controllers respect. In Indonesia, the newness of the UUPDP, the need for implementing regulations, and the fact that the supervisory institution is still being set up mean that the practical exercise of rights is largely untested. Moreover, if exceptions for “public interest” or “state authority” are interpreted broadly, whole categories of processing by the state may be exempted from the full range of data subject rights, creating a significant divergence from the GDPR in practice even if the black-letter law looks similar.

## 4. Exceptions and Restrictions

**GDPR.** The Regulation allows member states to restrict the scope of obligations and rights when such restriction respects the essence of fundamental rights and is a necessary and proportionate measure in a democratic society for objectives such as national security, defence, public security, the prevention and prosecution of criminal offences, or other important objectives of general public interest. Such restrictions must be laid down in law. Processing for law enforcement purposes is governed by a separate directive (Directive 2016/680), which provides its own set of safeguards and limits. Processing for activities that fall outside the scope of Union law (e.g. national security) is excluded from the GDPR altogether and remains a matter for member state law, subject to the European Convention on Human Rights and the EU Charter. The result is a layered system: full GDPR protection for most processing; derogations only when explicitly provided by law; and a distinct regime for law enforcement, with national security in a category of its own.

**UUPDP.** Indonesia’s law also provides for exceptions and restrictions. Processing may be exempted or limited for reasons of national security, law enforcement, and other public interests. The exact wording and the conditions under which these exceptions apply are critical. If the exceptions are broad and loosely defined—for example, if “state interest” or “public order” can be invoked without clear legal definition or judicial oversight—then a large share of processing by public bodies could fall outside the full reach of the law. The UUPDP does not necessarily create a separate, detailed regime for law enforcement or national security equivalent to the EU’s directive and carve-outs; instead, it may leave considerable discretion to implementing regulations and to the practice of authorities.

**Comparison.** Here the comparison is most sensitive. The GDPR, for all its complexity, confines full exemptions to a narrow set of activities and insists that any restriction be prescribed by law and proportionate. The EU’s legal order also subjects member state measures to the scrutiny of the Court of Justice and the European Court of Human Rights. In Indonesia, the constitutional and judicial framework is different; the scope of “national security,” “public order,” or “state authority” in the context of data protection has yet to be tested. If the UUPDP’s exceptions are interpreted broadly, the gap between the two regimes will be substantial: the same formal rights and principles would apply in theory, but in practice a significant part of state processing could be placed beyond effective scrutiny. Comparative analysis therefore cannot stop at the text; it must ask how the exceptions will be used and who will have the final say on their scope.

## 5. Institutional Design and Enforcement

**GDPR.** Each member state must establish one or more independent supervisory authorities with the power to investigate complaints, conduct audits, order compliance, and impose administrative fines. The independence of these authorities is a key requirement: they must act without external influence and have sufficient resources. For cross-border processing, the “one-stop shop” mechanism allocates a lead supervisory authority, with the European Data Protection Board ensuring consistency. Fines can reach up to €20 million or 4% of the undertaking’s total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher. Data subjects have the right to lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority and to an effective judicial remedy against the authority and against the controller or processor. Collective redress and representation are possible. The combination of strong investigative and corrective powers, high fines, and accessible complaint mechanisms has made the GDPR a deterrent in many sectors.

**UUPDP.** The Indonesian law establishes a supervisory institution for personal data protection. Its exact name, structure, and placement within the state administration may be specified in implementing regulations. The law provides for administrative sanctions (warnings, fines, suspension or termination of processing), as well as civil liability and criminal penalties for certain violations. The independence of this institution from the executive and from other data controllers (including government bodies) will be crucial. If the authority is under the direct control of a ministry that is also a major data controller, or if its budget and appointments are subject to political interference, its ability to enforce the law against the state or against powerful private actors will be limited. The level of fines and the ease with which data subjects can lodge complaints and obtain redress will also shape the real impact of the law.

**Comparison.** The GDPR’s enforcement model rests on independent authorities with real powers and on the possibility of severe fines. That model is not perfect—resources vary, and not all complaints lead to sanctions—but it has created a credible threat of enforcement. The UUPDP’s institutional design is still taking shape. The legal issue is whether Indonesia will converge toward a strong, independent supervisory body with adequate powers and resources, or whether the new institution will remain weak or subordinated to other interests. The same applies to sanctions: if fines are low and rarely imposed, and if criminal provisions are not used, the deterrent effect will be limited. Comparative analysis thus highlights not only the formal structure of the UUPDP but the need to monitor how the supervisory institution is established, how it behaves, and how often it acts against both private and public controllers.

---

## Synthesis

The analysis across the five dimensions shows a pattern: the GDPR and the UUPDP converge at the level of principles and of the catalogue of rights and obligations. Both require a lawful basis for processing, both recognise data subject rights to access, rectification, erasure, and the rest, and both provide for a supervisory authority and for sanctions. The divergences appear when one looks at territorial scope, at the interpretation of consent and legitimate interest in the shadow of state power, at the breadth of exceptions and restrictions, and at the design and practice of enforcement. Indonesia has adopted a law that is clearly inspired by the GDPR, but “inspired” does not mean “identical.” The next section draws conclusions from this comparison and reflects on its implications for the future of data protection in Indonesia and for the global debate on the transfer and adaptation of data protection norms.
